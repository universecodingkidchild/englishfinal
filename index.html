Part One , Gather

Gather your knowledge

The activities that stayed with me , and what the novel changed in how I understand Indigenous experiences in Canada.

Activity 01

Finding myself in a character

While I can't exactly resonate with anyone from the story , I'm lucky enough to have not suffered abuse like the characters did , the closest I can relate to is Kenny. Kenny has a tendency to roam, to get up and leave, and I can kind of relate to that. Sometimes I just get up, look around, and decide to leave. It sets my mind free and makes me reflect on life and the choices I make. It's similar to Kenny, although when he gets up and leaves, we don't exactly know what's going through his head.

Activity 02

Connecting the story to the real world

This activity made me connect the novel to the real history of residential schools in Canada , the Truth and Reconciliation Commission, and the unmarked graves found in 2021, like at Kamloops. The book mattered because it made all of that feel personal instead of just facts. You actually see what the schools did to real kids, and what it left behind.

Reflection

What the novel helped me understand

Before reading Five Little Indians, I thought residential schools were just a place where kids were oppressed, but this book changed that. I now know how they were horrid places, with rape, assault, and even homicide, and many children who never made it out. It completely changed how I see residential schools and what Indigenous people went through. I feel awful for all the kids who didn't survive, and it's not right that the full truth only started coming out decades later.

Part Two , Build

Build your knowledge

The advocate I signed up to explore , Lily Gladstone , and why I think you should know about her.

Profile

Who she is

Lily Gladstone is an actor of Blackfeet and Nez Perce heritage who grew up on the Blackfeet Reservation in Montana. Her breakout role was playing Mollie Burkhart, an Osage woman, in Killers of the Flower Moon (2023), which made her the first Native American to win a Golden Globe for Best Actress and the first to be nominated for the Best Actress Oscar. She uses she/they pronouns.

The reason I picked her isn't the awards , it's what she does with the attention they brought. At the Golden Globes she gave part of her speech in the Blackfeet language, the first time it had ever been spoken on that stage, a language her mom had fought to get taught in their schools. She's narrated a documentary about her nation bringing wild buffalo back to their land, hosted educational videos on the history of Indigenous women and the violence they still face, and given talks on Indigenous sovereignty and cultural survival.

Takeaway

What my peers should know

Representation actually changes things , when she spoke Blackfeet at an awards show, kids on her reservation saw their own language treated as something to be proud of, on a huge stage. She's said she's the first, but won't be the last.

And even though she's American, the issues she speaks on aren't only American. The Blackfoot Confederacy stretches across the border into Alberta, and the things she fights for , language loss, the boarding and residential school legacy, missing and murdered Indigenous women, getting back land and culture , are the exact same wounds at the center of Five Little Indians.

Reflection

How it shifted me

Looking into Lily Gladstone shifted my perspective again, but this time for the better. The book left me feeling awful, like residential schools were just this horrible thing that happened to Indigenous people and that was the end of it. Gladstone showed me the other side , that Indigenous people aren't just victims of the past, they're still here, fighting back and reclaiming what was taken from them. Seeing her speak her own language on a huge stage, and use her fame to put Indigenous history and issues in front of people who'd never see them otherwise, made me realize the story isn't over. It's not only damage anymore , it's also survival and pride.

Part Three , Share

Share your knowledge

What I'm bringing into the circle.

Closing

What I want everyone to take away is that residential schools weren't just a place kids got "oppressed" , they were horrible places where real children suffered and many never made it home, and the truth only came out decades later. That's the part the book made real for me. But I also don't want to leave it there. People like Lily Gladstone show that Indigenous people are still here, reclaiming their language and culture and refusing to be forgotten. The one thing I'm still sitting with is how recently all of this happened, and how it's not really "history" , it's still going on, and the least we can do is actually listen.
